## The Problem

Madrid's rental market is not a single market. It is a collection of structurally distinct sub-markets where identical square meters command wildly different rents depending on district, floor level, and building type. A blended pricing model conflates these segments and systematically misprices both luxury and budget properties.

This project answers two questions for every listing: **which market segment does it belong to?** and **what should it rent for?** Phase 1 discovers the market structure through unsupervised clustering. Phase 2 builds a segment-specific pricing model that captures how location, size, and amenities interact differently across neighborhoods.

## Data

2,089 rental listings from Idealista.com across Madrid, with 15 variables covering structural attributes (square meters, bedrooms, floor), amenities (elevator, exterior, penthouse, cottage, duplex, semi-detached), location (district), and the target variable (monthly rent in euros).

Data cleaning addressed five variables with missing values, three with extreme values, and one data-entry error (a floor value of 43,039). Outliers were capped using the IQR method, and missing values were imputed using contextual medians based on district and area to preserve neighborhood-specific building patterns.

## Phase 1: Clustering Segmentation

### Variable Selection

Seven candidate variables were evaluated. Rent and district were excluded from clustering: rent is the regression target, and district would distort segmentation toward pricing results. Multicollinearity screening revealed a strong correlation between bedrooms and square meters (r = 0.755, VIF ≈ 9), so only one could be kept.

Two combinations were tested across 12 scenarios: Bedrooms + Floor and Sq.Mt + Floor. Bedrooms + Floor was selected for two reasons: it delivered a 9.7% higher silhouette score (0.4552 vs. 0.4053), and it mirrors how tenants actually search for apartments.

Binary amenities (elevator, exterior, penthouse, cottage) were excluded from clustering because they produced "dominated clusters" where 99%+ of one group shared a single feature. Instead, amenities were used as profiling variables after segmentation.

### K Selection

K was evaluated from 2 to 7 using inertia, silhouette, Calinski-Harabasz, and Davies-Bouldin metrics. K = 3 delivered the highest silhouette (0.460), but it merges the Large High-Rise Executive and Large Low-Rise Family segments. K = 4 (silhouette 0.455) preserves the structural distinction between these two property types, and the 0.005 silhouette trade-off is commercially justified.

### The Four Segments

The market follows a 2x2 matrix defined by unit size (compact ≤ 2 beds vs. large ≥ 3 beds) and floor level (low-rise ≤ 4 vs. high-rise ≥ 5):

| Cluster | Profile | n (%) | Median Rent | €/m² |
|---------|---------|-------|-------------|------|
| **C0** | Large High-Rise Executive | 229 (11.0%) | €2,400 | €15.0 |
| **C1** | Compact Traditional | 903 (43.2%) | €1,100 | €16.2 |
| **C2** | Large Low-Rise Family | 624 (29.9%) | €2,300 | €13.7 |
| **C3** | Small High-Rise Modern | 333 (15.9%) | €1,400 | €17.7 |

All four clusters pass validation: only 1 out of 2,089 listings shows a negative silhouette score, cluster sizes are well balanced (11-43%), and rent dispersion is stable across segments (CV between 0.478 and 0.537).

### Business Interpretation

**C1 (Compact Traditional) is the volume segment.** At 43.2% of all listings, it anchors the market. An apartment in Centro costs €300-400 more per month than an identical one in Vallecas, despite a median rent of just €1,100. Its price intensity of €16.2/m² outperforms both larger-unit segments, confirming that tenants prioritize central location over space.

**C0 and C2 share a price point but are opposite products.** Both rent for €2,300-2,400/month, but C0 offers height, views, and luxury interiors (penthouse 19.7%, elevator 99.6%, Salamanca/Chamartin), while C2 provides garden access and suburban dimensions (cottage 13.1%, Moncloa/Hortaleza). Pricing them against a shared benchmark systematically misprices both.

**C3 vs. C1 reveals the floor premium.** Both clusters have a median of 2 bedrooms, yet C3 rents for €1,400/month vs. C1's €1,100. That 27.3% premium is attributable entirely to floor level (6.7 vs. 2.1) and building modernity. Benchmarking a C3 listing against a comparable C1 leaves roughly €300/month on the table.

## Phase 2: Regression Pricing Model

### Scope and Setup

The regression focuses on C1 (Compact Traditional), selected for three reasons: it represents 43.2% of all listings, it carries the highest within-segment rent dispersion (CV = 0.532, σ = €677), and its €16.2/m² price intensity points to a location-driven pricing pattern that a district-aware model can exploit.

Districts with fewer than 25 listings were grouped into an "Other" category to avoid unstable coefficients. One-hot encoding with a reference district was applied. The dataset was split into training (45%), testing (45%), and a reserved hold-out set (10%).

### Model Building

The baseline OLS model with all predictors achieved R² = 0.627. Bedrooms and Floor (the clustering variables) were dropped with virtually no R² loss (≈ 0.626), confirming their limited variability within the cluster. Feature selection via RFECV reduced the model to 17 predictors, and backward elimination further removed non-significant district dummies.

Interaction terms between Sq.Mt and District were introduced to allow the marginal effect of size to vary by location. This captures the spatial heterogeneity that a standard linear model misses.

### Final Model

The final model retains 10 predictors: Sq.Mt, Outer (exterior-facing), two district dummies (Other, Puente Vallecas), and six Sq.Mt × District interactions.

| Predictor | β | Significance |
|-----------|---|-------------|
| Intercept | €375.39 | *** |
| Sq.Mt (baseline) | €7.38/m² | *** |
| Outer (exterior) | +€107.27 | * |
| District: Other | -€214.98 | ** |
| District: Puente Vallecas | -€199.91 | * |
| Sq.Mt × Salamanca | +€10.85/m² | *** |
| Sq.Mt × Centro | +€6.03/m² | *** |
| Sq.Mt × Chamberí | +€5.78/m² | *** |
| Sq.Mt × Retiro | +€4.26/m² | *** |
| Sq.Mt × Chamartín | +€3.79/m² | *** |
| Sq.Mt × Moncloa | +€3.22/m² | * |

The key insight: **location dominates size.** In Salamanca, each additional m² is worth €18.23 in rent (baseline €7.38 + interaction €10.85), 2.5 times the rate in peripheral districts. A 70 m² flat in Salamanca is predicted at ~€1,650, vs. ~€890 in the reference district, an 85% location premium driven entirely by address. Compact Traditional tenants "pay for address, not space."

## Conclusions

**The Madrid rental market is segmented, not continuous.** Treating all listings as a single market creates systematic pricing errors. The segmentation identifies four discrete property types with distinct price dynamics.

**Where a property is located matters more than how big it is.** Within Compact Traditional, district drives rent more than floor area. A square meter in Salamanca is €10.85 above the base rate, nearly 2.5x the premium of a square meter in the average district.

**Segment-first pricing prevents errors a blended model cannot see.** Two segments (C0 and C2) occupy the same price range with opposite profiles. A single-market model averages them out and produces wrong estimates for both. Segmenting first ensures the right pricing logic is applied to the right property type.
